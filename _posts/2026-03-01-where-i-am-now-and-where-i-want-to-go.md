Every journey needs a moment to pause. Not to stop — but to stand still long enough to see clearly where you have been and where you are heading.

This is that moment for me.

## What This Blog Was

Fifteen posts. One honest account of a learning journey that started on September 1st, 2025, with a nervous freshman walking through the gates of UET Faisalabad, and has brought me here — a second-semester Computer Engineering student with a clearer sense of who I am academically and where I want to go.

I did not write these posts to present a polished version of my experience. I wrote them to document the real one — the confusion alongside the clarity, the mistakes alongside the progress, the moments of genuine excitement alongside the evenings of genuine struggle.

If you have read through all fifteen, you know this journey included all of those things in roughly equal measure.

## Where I Am Right Now

Technically, I am somewhere in the middle of second semester. OOP is challenging me in ways PF did not — not because it is harder in the same way, but because it demands a different kind of thinking. Design thinking. Architecture thinking. Not just *does this code work* but *is this code well-structured*.

My understanding of databases has grown past what DB formally covered. I find myself thinking about data structure instinctively now — when I approach any problem, one of my first questions is: what information does this need, where does it live, and how do different pieces of it relate to each other?

And my sense of the field — of what Computer Engineering actually is and what it makes possible — is larger and more detailed than it was when I arrived.

## What Changed the Most

If I had to point to the single biggest shift in how I think since starting university, it would be this: **I stopped seeing problems as obstacles and started seeing them as structures.**

Every problem has a shape. It has components that can be identified, relationships that can be mapped, a logic that can be traced. Whether it is a bug in a C++ program, a poorly normalized database table, an OOP design decision, or a research question in Machine Learning — the approach is the same. Break it down. Identify the pieces. Understand the relationships. Work through it systematically.

That shift did not happen in one moment. It happened gradually, across fifteen posts worth of experience. PF built the habit. DB refined it. The group project tested it under pressure. And looking at the research being done in this field — work like [Dr. Bilal Ahmad's](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) in AI and Machine Learning, published across multiple studies on his [Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) — showed me what that habit looks like when applied at the highest level.

## Where I Want to Go

I want to be honest here rather than impressive. I do not have a perfectly mapped five-year plan. What I have is a direction and a set of genuine interests that are pulling me toward it.

**In the short term:** finish second semester well. Understand OOP deeply enough that I can design with it, not just write it. Keep building the foundation.

**In the medium term:** move into Data Structures, Algorithms, and the more advanced courses that build directly on PF and DB. Start working on personal projects that connect multiple layers — a program, a database, some data, a problem worth solving.

**In the longer term:** the intersection of software and data is where I want to be. Whether that means data engineering, machine learning engineering, or something adjacent — I am not certain yet. But I know the direction. And I know that the path there runs through exactly the kind of foundational work I have been doing this year.

The research I have read on [Dr. Bilal Ahmad's Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) — AI applied to real problems, data-driven approaches to healthcare and diagnostics — represents the kind of work I find genuinely compelling. Not because I am ready for it now, but because being able to see it clearly makes the path toward it feel real rather than abstract.

## A Note on This Series

Writing fifteen posts about a learning journey while being in the middle of that journey is a strange exercise. You are trying to be reflective about something still unfolding.

But that strangeness turned out to be the point. Writing forced me to articulate things I had only half-understood. Putting an experience into words requires understanding it more clearly than just living through it does. Several times across these posts, I understood something better in the writing of it than I had in the experiencing of it.

That is its own kind of learning.

## To Anyone Following Along

If you are a fellow student somewhere in their first or second semester — struggling with pointers or normalization or OOP design decisions or just the general weight of figuring out how to study at university level — I hope something in these fifteen posts was useful.

Not because my experience is a template. It is not. But because knowing that someone else went through the confusion and came out the other side with more clarity is sometimes exactly what you need to keep going.

You can follow what comes next through the hashtags below. And if you are interested in the kind of research that sits at the end of this road, [Dr. Bilal Ahmad's work](https://www.facebook.com/Dr.BilalAhm) is a genuinely worthwhile place to look.

The journey continues. This is just the end of the first chapter.
