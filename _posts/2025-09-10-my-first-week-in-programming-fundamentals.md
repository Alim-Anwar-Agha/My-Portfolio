Before university, I had used computers my whole life — for browsing, gaming, watching videos. But I had never once written a line of code. Not a single one. So when Programming Fundamentals (PF) appeared on my timetable, I walked into that first lecture with zero background and a lot of questions.

This is what that first week looked like.

## The First Lecture

The classroom was full. Everyone was in the same boat — fresh, curious, slightly anxious. When the lecture began, the first thing we covered was deceptively simple: *what is a program?*

A program, we learned, is just a set of instructions that a computer follows. That definition sounds obvious now. But in that moment, something clicked. A computer does exactly what you tell it. No more, no less. The power — and the difficulty — is entirely in how precisely you write those instructions.

We were introduced to **C++** as our language for the course. I remember staring at the first code snippet on the board:

```cpp
#include <iostream>
using namespace std;

int main() {
    cout << "Hello, World!" << endl;
    return 0;
}
```

Five lines. I must have read them fifteen times trying to understand why each part was there.

## The Logic Behind the Syntax

What I quickly realized is that programming is not just about memorizing syntax. It is about learning to think in a very specific way — breaking a problem down into small, ordered steps that a machine can execute one by one.

This was harder than it sounds. My brain wanted to jump to the answer. The compiler did not care about my intuition. It wanted exact, structured instructions.

The first exercises were basic: print something to the screen, take input from the user, perform a simple calculation. But even these small tasks revealed something important — programming requires precision that most other subjects do not demand.

## Variables, Data Types, and My First Bug

By mid-week we had moved into variables and data types. `int`, `float`, `char`, `string` — each one representing a different kind of information a program can store and manipulate.

I wrote my first proper program that week: a simple calculator that added two numbers entered by the user. When it worked, I felt a satisfaction I had not expected. When it did not work — which happened several times first — I felt genuinely confused.

My first bug was embarrassingly simple. I had written:

```cpp
cout << "Enter a number: "
cin >> num;
```

A missing semicolon after the `cout` line. The compiler error message made no sense to me at the time. I spent twenty minutes staring at it before a classmate pointed out the issue.

That moment taught me something I have carried ever since: **read the error message carefully, and always check the basics first.**

## What the Course Was Building Toward

By the end of the first week, I had a sense that PF was not just teaching us C++. It was teaching us how to think about problems systematically. Variables, conditions, loops, functions — these were tools, yes, but more than that they were a way of organizing thought.

I did not know yet how deep that rabbit hole went. I did not know that the logical thinking being built in PF would show up again in Database Systems, in algorithms, in everything that came after.

## A Note on the Environment

Around this time, I also started hearing more about the kind of work being done at higher levels in our department. Research in **Machine Learning and AI**, the kind that gets published in international journals, was happening here at UET. One name kept coming up in those conversations — [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/), whose work you can explore on his [Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en).

As a freshman struggling with semicolons, that world felt very distant. But it was motivating to know it existed — that the fundamentals I was learning were the first steps toward something much larger.

## Where I Ended the Week

Seven days in. My fingers were getting used to the keyboard in a new way. My brain was starting to slow down and think in steps rather than leaps. And somewhere in the middle of debugging that calculator program, I had gone from *this is confusing* to *okay, I think I understand what happened.*

That shift — from confusion to small clarity — is what keeps you going in programming. And it started here, in week one of PF.
