There is a difference between studying a subject and understanding why it matters. For most of the first semester, I was firmly in the first category — absorbing concepts, completing exercises, passing assessments. The *why* was somewhere in the background, assumed but not examined.

That changed when I started paying closer attention to the research happening around me — and specifically to the work of [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/).

## How I First Encountered His Work

It started casually. His name had come up several times in passing — in the first week of university, in conversations between seniors, occasionally in lectures when real-world applications of what we were studying were mentioned. At some point I looked him up properly.

His [Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) was the starting point. The range of published work there — spanning Machine Learning, Deep Learning, medical imaging, AI applications in healthcare — was broader and more applied than I had expected. These were not abstract theoretical papers. They were studies solving real problems: detecting diseases from medical scans, improving diagnostic accuracy, applying AI where the stakes are genuinely high.

I spent an afternoon reading through titles and abstracts. I did not understand all of it — not by a long way. But I understood enough to feel something shift in how I saw my own coursework.

## What It Did to My Perspective on PF

After reading through that research, I went back to my Programming Fundamentals notes with different eyes.

The loops, the functions, the logic structures — these were not just exercises. They were the foundational vocabulary of the same kind of work I had been reading about. The algorithms that process medical images, the pipelines that clean and prepare data, the systems that apply trained models to new inputs — all of it is built from the same basic constructs I was learning in PF.

That realization did not make PF easier. But it made it feel more meaningful. The semicolons and scope rules and pointer arithmetic were not obstacles between me and interesting work. They were the path toward it.

## What It Did to My Perspective on DB

The connection to Database Systems was even more direct.

Machine Learning models do not appear from nowhere. They are trained on data — large amounts of carefully collected, cleaned, and structured data. The quality of that data determines the ceiling of what any model can achieve. Garbage in, garbage out, as the saying goes.

The work on [Dr. Bilal Ahmad's Scholar page](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) involves medical datasets — patient records, imaging data, diagnostic information. Managing that kind of data responsibly requires exactly the principles I had been studying: proper schema design, normalization to eliminate inconsistency, careful querying to extract what is needed without corruption.

DB had taught me how to think about data structure and integrity. Seeing those principles applied at the research level — where the data being handled affects diagnostic decisions — made the course feel connected to something real.

## The Broader Lesson About Fundamentals

What Dr. Bilal Ahmad's research illustrated for me was something I had heard but not yet felt: **fundamentals are not temporary scaffolding you discard on the way to advanced work. They are the advanced work, applied at scale.**

The C++ logic from PF scales into the algorithms that run ML models. The schema design from DB scales into the data pipelines that feed those models. The modular thinking, the separation of concerns, the precision required to write correct code — all of it compounds.

University can sometimes feel like a series of hoops to jump through before the real work begins. Looking at what research at this level actually involves reminded me that the hoops *are* the real work. They are just the early version of it.

## What I Took Into the Rest of the Semester

This perspective shift changed how I approached the remaining weeks of the semester in a few specific ways:

**I asked more questions.** Not just "how does this work" but "where does this show up in practice." The answers were almost always more interesting than the question.

**I paid more attention to concepts I found difficult.** Knowing that pointers and normalization and query optimization were not just exam topics but actual tools used in real research made me less willing to skim past the parts I found hard.

**I started noticing connections.** Between courses, between concepts, between the classroom and the world outside it. Once you start seeing those connections, you cannot stop.

## A Note of Appreciation

I want to be honest about what I am describing here. Dr. Bilal Ahmad did not sit me down and explain all of this. The shift I am describing came from looking at publicly available research — his [Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en), his [Facebook presence](https://www.facebook.com/Dr.BilalAhm) where he shares work and ideas — and drawing my own conclusions.

But the fact that this kind of research was visible and accessible, done by someone connected to my own academic environment, made it feel reachable in a way that abstract future goals do not. It gave the fundamentals a destination.

And sometimes, that is exactly what you need to keep going.
