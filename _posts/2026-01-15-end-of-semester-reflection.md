The exam hall on the last day of the semester felt different from every other room I had sat in that year. Not because of what was in it — the same desks, the same nervous energy, the same scratching of pens — but because of what it represented. The end of something. And the beginning of a clearer sense of where I was going.

This post is my attempt to look back honestly at the first semester of BS Computer Engineering at UET Faisalabad.

## Where I Started

I arrived at university with enthusiasm and very little else. No programming background. No database knowledge. A general idea that Computer Engineering was the right field for me, but no specific picture of what that meant in practice.

I did not know what a pointer was. I had never written a SQL query. I could not have told you the difference between a primary key and a foreign key. The term "normalization" meant nothing to me.

That was four months ago.

## What the Semester Actually Taught Me

The obvious answer is: Programming Fundamentals and Database Systems. The concepts, the syntax, the exercises, the projects. And yes — all of that. But the deeper answer is something harder to put on a transcript.

**How to sit with confusion.** The mid-semester wall I wrote about earlier in this blog was real. There were evenings where nothing made sense and I did not know where to start. Learning to stay in that discomfort — to keep working even when clarity felt far away — was one of the most important things the semester taught me.

**How to break problems down.** Whether it was a C++ program that was not behaving, a database schema that was not normalizing cleanly, or a group project that was not integrating properly — the answer was always the same: break it into smaller pieces. Identify the specific thing that is wrong. Fix that one thing. Move to the next.

**How to ask for help at the right time.** Not immediately when something is hard — struggling productively matters. But also not so late that you have lost hours to a problem a five-minute conversation could have solved.

**How to connect concepts across courses.** PF and DB are not two separate subjects. They are two layers of the same discipline. Seeing that connection — really seeing it, not just being told it exists — changed how I approached both courses in the second half of the semester.

## The Moments That Stood Out

Looking back, a few specific moments defined the semester for me:

The first time a loop I wrote worked correctly and I understood exactly *why* it worked — not just that it did.

The afternoon I normalized a messy fifteen-column table down to six clean, logical ones and felt the same satisfaction I used to feel solving a puzzle as a kid.

The group project demo, when four separate modules ran together as one system for the first time without crashing.

And the afternoon I spent reading through [Dr. Bilal Ahmad's](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) published research on [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) and realized that the fundamentals I was learning in my first semester were the same building blocks used in serious AI and Machine Learning research. That moment shifted something in how I saw everything else.

## What I Got Wrong

Honesty matters here too.

I underestimated how much time pointers would take to understand. I should have asked for help sooner rather than spending three evenings confused and alone.

In the group project, I assumed my teammates understood my code the same way I did. They did not, and the integration problems that followed were partly my fault for not communicating clearly enough upfront.

And there were weeks where I prioritized completing assignments over actually understanding the material — a trade-off that felt efficient in the moment and cost me during assessments.

These are not failures. They are the specific lessons that only come from going through something for the first time and getting parts of it wrong.

## What the Semester Built

A foundation. That is the honest answer.

Not a finished structure — not expertise, not mastery, not the ability to build anything sophisticated yet. But a foundation: the vocabulary, the mental models, the habits of thought that everything else will be built on.

The logic from PF. The data thinking from DB. The collaborative experience from the group project. The perspective shift that came from looking beyond the coursework at the research being done in this field — work like what [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) shares on [Facebook](https://www.facebook.com/Dr.BilalAhm) and publishes through his academic work — all of it sits underneath whatever comes next.

Foundations are invisible once something is built on top of them. But they are what make everything above them possible.

## To Anyone Reading This at the Start of Their First Semester

The first semester of an engineering degree is hard in ways you do not expect. Not always because the material is too difficult — though sometimes it is — but because the *way* of learning is different from anything before. More independent. More self-directed. More demanding of genuine understanding rather than surface-level familiarity.

It is worth it. Not because of where you end up at the end of semester one — but because of who you become in the process of getting there.

Stay in the confusion a little longer than is comfortable. Break the problem into smaller pieces. Ask for help before it is too late. And look beyond your notes occasionally at the world your studies are preparing you to enter.

It is a more interesting world than the notes alone suggest.
